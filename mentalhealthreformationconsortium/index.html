    The Calling Sheet

    On a film set, the document that runs the day is the call sheet — a logistical instrument that tells each person when to arrive, where to stand, and what’s required of them. It is the paperwork of a zero-sum world: time is scarce, attention is rationed, and every name is reduced to a function and a call time. It calls people to work.

    We keep the discipline a call sheet provides — the gathering still has a place and an hour — but we refuse its register, because what we are making is not extracted from people; it is offered by them. So we call ours a Calling Sheet.

    A calling sheet does not summon a crew to a slot; it names what each person is called toward — the gift only they can bring to the pot. Where a call sheet lists roles, a calling sheet lists callings: not “the Lieutenant Governor, 8 a.m.,” but a whole human being arriving with an ingredient no one else carries.

    It is the Stone Soup made into a document — proof, on a single page, that the feast was never about the stone, and never about the call time. It was always about who chose to come, and what they chose to bring.
